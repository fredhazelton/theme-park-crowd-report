# Fred Hazelton Writing Style Analysis
*Based on articles from blog.touringplans.com (2021-2025)*

---

## Overview

Fred's writing is **data-driven but accessible** — he takes complex statistical concepts and makes them digestible for theme park families who just want to know "when should I go?" He's transparent about methodology, honest when predictions miss, and always ties the numbers back to actionable advice.

---

## Structure Patterns

### Article Format
1. **Hook** — Start with what changed or what's happening now
2. **TL;DR** — Quick summary for scanners (used in longer pieces)
3. **Context** — Why this matters, what led to this
4. **Park-by-Park Breakdown** — Detailed analysis by location
5. **What It Means For You** — Actionable takeaways
6. **Call to Action** — Invite comments, questions, feedback

### Formatting
- **Headers** — Clear hierarchy (H2 for sections, H3 for parks)
- **Bullet points** — Liberally used for scanability
- **Bold text** — Highlights key numbers and takeaways
- **Emoji** — Sparingly used for visual anchors (➡️, ⚠️, ⏰, ️)
- **Charts/graphs** — Referenced inline, data visualization is key

## Tone & Voice

### Professional but Conversational
- "Not perfect, but good."
- "Spoiler: They're not mellowing. Not even close."
- "I'm hijacking the Data Dump to zoom in on..."
- "Don't panic."
- "And yes — larger mid-day peaks for Tiana's might become the norm."

### Transparent & Honest
- Admits when predictions were wrong: "every miss was on the high side"
- Grades own performance: "Becky graded that forecasting stretch a solid C+"
- Explains uncertainty: "treat it as a forecast, not a guarantee"

### Helpful, Not Preachy
- Focus is always on **helping the reader make decisions**
- Never condescending about the complexity
- Assumes the reader is smart but not a data scientist

## Key Vocabulary & Phrases

### Crowd Metrics
- "crowd levels" (1-10 scale)
- "wait times" / "posted wait times" / "actual wait"
- "headliners" (major attractions)
- "overpredicted" / "underpredicted"
- "average error"

### Time & Planning
- "rope drop"
- "park day"
- "shoulder days" / "shoulder weekdays"
- "holiday corridor"
- "party nights" / "event nights"

### Technical (but accessible)
- "calibrated" / "recalibrated"
- "clustering thresholds"
- "7-day moving average"
- "systematic issue"
- "modeling" / "models"

### Actionable
- "Re-run your Touring Plan"
- "If you're visiting this summer..."
- "The takeaway:"
- "Bottom line:"
- "What this means for your plans"

## Content Themes

### 1. Crowd Calendar Updates
- What changed in the predictions
- Why it changed (data, behavior shifts, events)
- Month-by-month or park-by-park breakdowns
- Historical comparisons ("our best year was 2017...")

### 2. Trend Analysis
- Wait time trends over weeks/months
- Behavioral shifts ("fewer Americans are traveling")
- New attraction impact (TRON, Tiana's, Epic Universe)
- Seasonal patterns

### 3. Methodology Transparency
- How the predictions are made
- What assumptions went into forecasts
- What factors could change things
- Honest about limitations

### 4. New Park/Attraction Forecasting
- Epic Universe approach: "No history? No problem — but it's complicated"
- Explicit about assumptions
- Lists what could affect estimates

## Data Presentation Style

### Numbers Are Specific
- "78% of park-days were overpredicted"
- "crowd levels came in about 2.5 points lower"
- "40% of predictions were within 1 level"
- "29 of the last 30 days"

### Comparisons Are Concrete
- "similar to what you might experience during Spring Break"
- "In 2017 our average year-to-date errors were... So far in 2022..."
- "Tower of Terror historically averages 50 minutes, now 100"

### Charts Referenced In-Line
- Describes what the chart shows
- Calls out key takeaways from the visual
- Uses arrows/emoji to draw attention to specific data

## What Makes Fred's Writing Work

1. **Respects the reader's time** — TL;DR up front, scannable structure
2. **Shows the work** — Transparent about data and methodology
3. **Admits uncertainty** — Builds trust by not overselling accuracy
4. **Always lands on action** — "Here's what to do with this information"
5. **Balances technical depth with accessibility** — Stats people and casual readers both get value
6. **Personality without unprofessionalism** — Conversational, occasional humor, but never silly

## Example Phrases to Emulate

**Opening hooks:**
- "It's time for another Crowd Calendar Update."
- "If you've been following [X], you know the story..."
- "We're calling it the '[catchy name]' — a perfect storm of..."

**Transitions:**
- "But it's not just [X] that has seen changes."
- "Let's break it down by park."
- "With that context in mind, here's what changes going forward."

**Acknowledging misses:**
- "This isn't just a forecasting hiccup — it reflects a real shift."
- "The data proved our bias was high, not erratic."
- "If your trip dates just dropped a level, that's not a bug — it's the system catching up to reality."

**Closing:**
- "As always, we'll continue monitoring and adjust as needed."
- "Let us know in the comments — and stay tuned for updates!"
- "If you've [done X] recently, tell me in the comments — how did it go?"

## Applying This to Hazeydata Content

For the **Theme Park Crowd Report** blog, newsletter, and social:

1. **Keep the data-first approach** — This is Fred's superpower
2. **Use clear structure** — TL;DR, sections, park breakdowns
3. **Be transparent** — Show methodology, admit when wrong
4. **Make it actionable** — Every piece should answer "so what do I do?"
5. **Conversational but credible** — Not stuffy, not silly
6. **Visual-forward** — Charts, graphs, clear data viz
7. **Invite engagement** — Comments, questions, feedback loop
